Not Today, Patriarchy

Being a baddie was never just about the fits and the one-liners. Riya's version of it came with a spine. The kind that clocked a sexist comment the second it left someone's mouth, and did not let it slide just because it was said with a laugh attached.

She never needed a stage or a speech for it either. It showed up in the everyday stuff. Calling out the double standard when a man got praised for the exact thing a woman got side-eyed for. Refusing to shrink herself to make a room more comfortable. Backing up every woman in that office the second she sensed someone talking down to them, no questions asked, no hesitation.

That is the part people mistake for attitude. It was never attitude. It was standards. She simply refused to accept less respect than she gave, and somehow made the rest of us braver just by watching her do it.

Slay queen was never just an aesthetic with her. It came with substance, every single time.

The Soft Side Nobody Clocks

Here is the thing about a real baddie: the softest hearts hide behind the loudest confidence.

Riya is, before anything else, a sister. Watch her talk about Ritika for five seconds and the whole "I don't catch feelings" act disappears completely. She lights up like there is no bigger flex in the world than being someone's big sister. The check-in texts, the "eat kar liya?", the way she would drop everything if Ritika needed her, that is not performance, that is just who she is at the core.

I have never met Ritika. There is not a single photo of the two of them on this page. But I do not need one, because I have watched Riya's face change when her name comes up. I have seen her step out mid-conversation because Ritika called. I have heard her voice go softer, just slightly, every single time. You do not need a picture to know what real love looks like when you have watched it happen in real time, over and over again, across a desk.

That is the part that gets me the most, honestly. Someone can be the loudest, funniest, most unbothered person in the room, and still quietly build their whole world around making sure their little sister feels safe. Riya did both at once, every day, and never once made it feel like a contradiction.

It shows up everywhere else too. The way she cannot walk past a stray dog without stopping. The way she remembers your bad day three weeks later and still asks about it. The way she makes people feel held without ever turning it into a whole thing.

तू खूप प्रेमळ आहेस, आणि ते तुझ्या प्रत्येक कृतीतून दिसतं.

(You are so full of love, and it shows in everything you do.)

I am away from my own younger sibling, and somehow sitting next to Riya at work made me feel like home. Maybe that is because I recognized something in her, the way someone carries another person's happiness like it is their own responsibility. She made me cooler, braver, and honestly, a lot less uncle. I will miss the tea sessions more than I can put into words. Who am I supposed to go bitch to now?

Us, At the Office

Some people make a workplace bearable. Riya made it one of the best parts of my day.

The Divyam lunches. Every single one of them. Somehow a forty five minute lunch break turned into the highlight of the week, half the time not even talking about anything important, just existing together, which honestly was the whole point.

And the food orders, the completely random, makes no sense but we are doing it anyway orders. The kind where someone says one word at 4pm and suddenly the whole team is craving it. Nobody will ever hit share cart with the same chaotic energy again.

I am going to miss her in the ordinary ways more than the big ones. The desk that will feel too quiet. The message I will type out of habit before remembering there is no one to send it to at that desk anymore. The specific kind of missing that only shows up on a random Tuesday for no reason at all.

And genuinely, who is going to refill my bottle now? It sounds like nothing until it is gone. That small, unasked, no-big-deal habit of her getting up and just doing it without either of us ever mentioning it out loud. That is the stuff nobody puts in a farewell speech, but it is the stuff you actually miss the most.
